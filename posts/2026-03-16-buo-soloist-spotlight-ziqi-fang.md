For the Brown University Orchestra’s upcoming spring concert, the spotlight turns to Ziqi Fang, a versatile vocal baritone and violinist who will perform “Selections from Five Mystical Songs” (1911) by Ralph Vaughan Williams on March 14. A senior balancing the technical rigors of a major in Computer Science with a deep-seated passion for music, Ziqi is preparing for the next chapter of his musical journey as he applies to graduate vocal programs. In a recent conversation, Ziqi shared his evolution from dedicated instrumentalist to vocal soloist.

**A Journey to the Individual Voice**

While Ziqi has been a devoted violinist since middle school, his journey as a vocalist began in the joyful setting of elementary school choirs. This interest in choral singing continued through high school and followed him to Brown, where he decided to join the Brown University Chorus.

During his first semester at Brown, while performing the "Tuba Mirum" bass solo in Mozart’s Requiem, Ziqi had a moment of clarity. "That’s the moment when I was like, 'I actually really love singing,'" he recalled. This realization led him to the Applied Music Program (AMP), where he began formal lessons and started to truly embrace his own voice.

Ziqi’s love for singing deepened further in fall 2024 when he studied abroad in Paris. Singing with the Orchestra of Paris and working under world-renowned conductors provided him a glimpse into the life of a professional musician. "Meeting so many people who do music as their profession really inspired me," Ziqi said, noting that he decided then to pursue music as a career.

**C﻿onnecting with the Vaughan Williams**

The Five Mystical Songs by Ralph Vaughan Williams first captured Ziqi’s  interest when he sang it as a member of a chamber choir in Providence last spring. Though he wasn’t the soloist then, the magical and moving quality of the music stayed with him, sparking a desire to perform the work as a soloist with an orchestra.

Beyond the melody, Ziqi felt profound personal connection to the composer’s background and the spiritual depth of the work. Vaughan Williams identified as an atheist when he wrote the piece, yet he was deeply inspired by spirituality in English literature. He turned to the sacred poems of George Herbert to convey spiritual themes, a creative choice that resonates deeply with Ziqi’s own upbringing.

"I grew up in a very atheist part of China with an atheist family, so I didn’t have a lot of religion in my life growing up," Ziqi explained. "But because I sing a lot of early music and religious music, I find that I’m still able to convey so much emotion, and I feel like I live in that spiritual world". This ability to set sacred texts so effectively despite a secular background is exactly what Ziqi strives to emulate in his own work.

**C﻿elebration of Joy**

When performing, Ziqi balances technical precision with vivid mental imagery. For the fourth movement, he pictures the "calmness of the ocean," imagining each repetition of the melody as a new wave rolling in and bringing fresh perspective to the phrase. He views the work as an overall celebration of joy — one that is heightened by the power in the “collective voices” of the instrumentalists. While the final movement was originally written for choir, Ziqi is performing the version for solo baritone. "Singing as a solo baritone is like the role of the individual voice in that celebration," Ziqi said. "This piece is about joy and celebration, but also moments of meditation and reflection".

**R﻿eflections on the BUO Community**

As he nears graduation, Ziqi reflects on the four years he spent with the BUO with immense gratitude. For him, the orchestra has been an irreplaceable source of support and love throughout his undergraduate journey.

"I have so much love for this community, so much love for everybody in it," he shared. While his time at Brown is coming to a close, he is eager to see the orchestra continue to thrive and is looking forward to the upcoming tour.
